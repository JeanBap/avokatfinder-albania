Contract Formation

The legal process by which parties establish binding agreements through offer, acceptance, and mutual consideration.

Definition

Contract formation is the legal process by which two or more parties establish a binding agreement through the meeting of their minds and the exchange of consideration. In Albania, contract law is governed primarily by the Civil Code, which establishes the fundamental principles of offer and acceptance, consideration, capacity, and legality. For a contract to be formed, there must be a clear offer by one party and unequivocal acceptance by another, both parties must intend to create legal relations, and the contract must be for a lawful purpose.

An offer in Albanian law is a definite proposal by one party (the offeror) that indicates a willingness to be bound by specified terms. An offer must be clear enough that acceptance creates a binding contract. A mere invitation to negotiate or a display of goods is not an offer but rather an invitation to make an offer. Once an offer is made, the offeror can revoke it at any time before acceptance unless the offeror has promised to keep the offer open or has otherwise limited their right to revoke. The offeree has a reasonable time to accept the offer; if they do not respond within a reasonable period, the offer lapses.

Acceptance in Albanian law must be unequivocal and unconditional agreement to the exact terms of the offer. Acceptance can be expressed through words or conduct that clearly indicates agreement. If the acceptance contains different or additional terms, it is considered a rejection of the offer and constitutes a counteroffer. Once valid acceptance occurs, a binding contract is formed, and both parties are obligated to perform their obligations under the contract. Acceptance can be revoked only if the revocation reaches the offeror before acceptance is effective.

Consideration is the exchange of value that supports a contract and distinguishes it from a mere gift or promise. In Albanian law, consideration must be given by both parties; each party must provide something of value in exchange for the other's performance. Consideration does not need to be equal in value, but it must be genuine. Failure of a party to provide promised consideration can be grounds for breach of contract. Some contracts, such as gifts, may not require consideration, but they still require formalities to be binding.

For certain types of contracts, Albanian law requires specific formalities such as writing, notarization, or registration. Real estate contracts, for example, must be in writing and often must be notarized. Failure to comply with required formalities renders the contract void. Parties to a contract should ensure that all essential terms are agreed upon before considering the contract formed, and written contracts are strongly recommended to avoid disputes over the terms.

Key Facts

  • Contract formation requires offer, acceptance, and mutual consideration.
  • An offer must be clear and definite; acceptance must be unconditional.
  • Acceptance creates a binding contract unless the contract requires specific formalities.
  • Consideration is an exchange of value required for most contracts in Albania.
  • Certain contracts, such as real estate transactions, require writing and notarization.

Common Mistake

People assume oral contracts are binding without realizing that certain contract types require writing or notarization to be valid.

Expert Tip

For important contracts, use written agreements and consult a lawyer to ensure the contract meets all legal requirements and contains all essential terms.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is required for a valid offer in Albania?

An offer must be a definite proposal indicating the offeror's willingness to be bound by specified terms.

What constitutes valid acceptance?

Acceptance must be unequivocal and unconditional agreement to the exact terms of the offer, expressed through words or conduct.

What contracts require writing in Albania?
