Regjistri i Pasurive (Property Register)

The official Albanian property register maintained by the State Agency for the Immovable Property Registration (AIPK/ZRPP), recording ownership and encumbrances on real estate.

The regjistri i pasurive (immovable property register) in Albania is maintained by the Agjencia Shtetërore e Kadastrës (State Cadastre Agency, ASK), successor to the former ZRPP. Every parcel of land and building in Albania should have an entry in the cadastral register that records the owner, area, boundaries, and any mortgages or legal encumbrances. Before purchasing property in Albania, obtaining a certified cadastral extract (certifikatë pronësie) is the essential first step of legal due diligence. This document confirms who the registered owner is and whether the property is free of charges.

Albania underwent significant property restitution and registration reforms since the 1990s, and property title history can be complex. Many properties changed ownership during privatisation and post-communist restitution, and not all historical claims have been fully resolved. It is therefore critical to trace the full ownership history of any property you intend to purchase, not just the current registration entry. Your Albanian lawyer (avokat) should conduct thorough due diligence beyond the current cadastral certificate to identify any potential competing claims or restitution proceedings.

The cadastral extract includes the property identification number (numri i pasurisë), the cadastral zone, the area in square metres, the type of property, and the registered owner. Any hypothekë (mortgage) or servitutë (easement) registered against the property will also appear. If the property has a hypothekë, the seller must arrange its formal cancellation (fshirje e hipotekës) before or at the time of transfer. Buying a property with an undisclosed or unresolved mortgage can result in the buyer inheriting the debt obligation.

The formal transfer of property ownership in Albania requires a notarially authenticated contract (kontratë shitblerje) prepared and executed before a notary (noter). The noteri verifies the identity of buyer and seller, confirms the cadastral extract, and authenticates the sale. After authentication, the contract must be registered with the State Cadastre Agency within a prescribed period. Registration fees in Albania are calculated based on the declared property value. Under-declaration of value to reduce fees is technically illegal but historically common — always declare the true purchase price to protect yourself legally.

Foreign nationals can generally purchase residential and commercial property in Albania, though agricultural and forest land purchases face restrictions for foreigners. EU citizens and citizens of countries with bilateral agreements with Albania typically have the same rights as Albanian nationals for non-agricultural purchases. Non-EU nationals should verify current restrictions with their avokat before proceeding, as regulations have been subject to change following Albania's EU accession candidacy process.

Key Facts

  • Maintained by State Cadastre Agency (ASK)
  • Certifikatë pronësie confirms current ownership and encumbrances
  • Ownership history can be complex due to post-communist restitution
  • All property transfers require notarial authentication (noter)
  • Foreign buyers may purchase residential property; agricultural land restricted

Common Mistake

Many buyers rely only on the current cadastral certificate without investigating the full ownership history. Given Albania's property restitution history, a current registration may co-exist with an unresolved restitution claim from a former owner. Your avokat should search court records and local administrative archives for any pending or historical claims, not just the current cadastral entry.

Practical Tip

Always commission a full ownership chain (historiku i pronës) from your avokat before making any financial commitment. Pay the full declared price through a bank transfer to create a paper trail — cash transactions are risky and provide no legal protection if the purchase is later challenged. Ensure the notarial contract specifies that the property is sold free of all encumbrances.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreigners buy property in Albania?

Yes, foreign nationals can generally purchase residential and commercial property in Albania. However, purchases of agricultural land and forest land face restrictions for non-Albanian nationals. EU citizens and citizens of countries with bilateral treaties typically enjoy the same rights as Albanian nationals for residential purchases. Always verify current rules with a local avokat.

What is a certifikatë pronësie?

It is the official cadastral certificate issued by the State Cadastre Agency (ASK) confirming who the registered owner of a property is. It also shows the property area, type, and any registered mortgages or easements. This document is required for property sales, mortgage applications, and inheritance proceedings.

How long does property registration take in Albania?

After the notarially authenticated purchase contract is prepared, registration with the State Cadastre Agency typically takes 5 to 30 working days depending on the local office workload and whether all documents are complete. Expedited procedures may be available for an additional fee.
